Equilibrium

The price determined in the economy is the equilibrium point between the demand curve and the supply curve.

equilibrium price - the price at the equilibrium point
equilibrium quantity - the quantity at the equilibrium point

We call this equilibrium because at any other price or quantity economic forces push prices towards the equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity

equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity are the only stable outcomes in an economy.

Adjustment Forces

surplus - situation in which quantity supplied is greater than quantity demanded

Let's say the equilibrium price of oil is $30, but the current price is $50. According to the quantity curve, sellers will supply 100 barrels of oil, but according to the demand curve consumers will only demand 32 barrels. This creates a surplus of 68 barrels. The competitors reason that in order to sell the surplus, they have to sell at a lower and lower price then each other, they then reach the equilibrium price of $30.

shortage - a situation in which quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied.

On the other hand, at a price of $15 demanders want 95, but suppliers only supply 24, creating a shortage of 71 barrels. The suppliers, looking at the shortage pump their prices up, because they know the demand is so high. They pump their prices up until demanders want less, and the shortage is fixedat the equilibrium price of $30.

The equilibrium price is stable because at the equilibrium the quantity demanded and the quantity supplied is equal.

At equilibrium, economic forces neither push nor pull prices and quantities so it is stable. Specifically talking about competitive markets, and in the real world there are regular supply and demand-curve shifts.

A common misconception is sellers compete with buyers, but the opposite is true. Sellers compete with sellers, and compete by lowering prices to attract customers. Buyers compete with other buyers, and compete by raising prices to attract sellers.

Producer Plus and Consumer Surplus

A free market maximizes both of these, and reduces wasted resources.

If for some reason suppliers produce a quantity over the equilibrium quantity in the oil market, they are spending more getting the oil then actually selling it for. For example, it costs them $50 to harvest oil from tar sands, but buyers only want to buy that extra last barrel for $15 barrels. What a waste! Not just to the suppliers, but to the society. Those trucks, those workers, and that time could have been spent on more beneficial affairs that society values more then $15.

A free market maximizes gains from trade

An Experiment

To prove that the model works, even if buyers and sellers don't know the true market curve, and experiment was ran by Vernon Smith:

  • Students were split into two groups, Buyers and Sellers
  • Buyers were given a card that indicated their maximum willingness to pay
  • Sellers were given a card indicating cost, their minimum price at which they were willing to sell.
  • Vernon knew the true curve, and knew the expected price and units expected.

Almost every single time, the price converged to something similar to the equilibrium price of 2.00 and equilibrium quantity of 6 units.

Total surplus was maximized, and there were very few instances where sellers sold above the market prices and buyers bought under it.

He initially used the experiments to try and disprove it, but the results aligned exactly with the model.
