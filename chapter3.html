Consumer Choice and Individual Demand

demand schedule - Function that shows the quantity demanded at different prices on a given market, holding everything else equal.

Individuals' demand curves reflect the question that people face everyday as a buyer:

  • At a price, how much should you buy?
    Based on three core economic ideas:
  • Marginal Principle
  • Cost benefit principle
  • Opportunity cost principle

EXAMPLE:
Darren's individual demand curve for gas

  • Darren is driving to the office and notices his gas tank is nearly empty
  • The gas station sells it at 2.99$
  • If Marginal Benefit >= Marginal Cost, then Darren buys it, so he buys it when his marginal benefit is above 3$.

Rational Rule for Buyers
Buy more of an item if its marginal benefit is greater than (or equal to) the price.

  • Using this info, we can create an individual demand curve

Big Caveat
When illustrating an individual demand curve, we ask how much we would be willing to purchase at each price, holding other things constant.

Market Demand

market demand curve - Graph plotting the total quantity of an item demanded, by the entire market, at each price. Sum of the quantity demanded by each person.

Reading a Demand Curve
  • Horizontally: At a given price, how much are people willing to buy
  • Vertically: What are people willing to pay for a given amount

EXAMPLE:

HORIZONTAL: At $20 per barrel, buyers are willing to buy to 25M barrels of oil per day.
VERTICAL: The maximum price that buyers are willing to pay to purchase 25M barrels per day is $20 per barrel

The Law of Demand

For nearly all goods, the demand curve slopes downward
The quality demanded is higher when the price is lower (holding all other things constant)

Price = marginal benefit
Demand curve = marginal benefit curve

Consumption comes with a diminishing marginal benefit—each additional unit yields a smaller marginal benefit than the previous unit.

Possibilities for Substitution
When the price is high, consumers will use it only in valuable uses with little opportunity for substitution.
As the price falls, consumers will also use oil in its less valued uses or those with ready substitutes.

Exceptions
Giffen goods - Cheap necessities, for which an increase in prices might cause consumers to buy more because they can no long afford luxury substitutes (potatoes in Famine-era Ireland). They need it.
Veblen goods - luxury goods whose value lays primarily in conspicuous consumption
Can be usually ignored.

Consumer Surplus

The consumer's gain from exchange; difference between the maximum price a consumer is willing to pay for a certain quantity and the market price.

Total consumer surplus - The area beneath the demand curve and above the price.

An increase in demand shifts the demand curve to the right.
At each price, people are willing to buy more
A decrease in demand shifts the demand curve to the left
At each price, people are willing to buy less.

Factors the Shift Demand

  1. Income
    1. When people get richer, they buy more stuff
    2. When an increase in income increases the demand for a good, it is a normal good.
    3. Most goods are normal goods.
      1. For example, those with low incomes might want to buy a TV, but can't afford it. When their income increases, they can buy it, and it increases demand for TVs.
      2. Target, a little more expensive but a little bit nicer
    4. When an increase in income decreases the demand for a good, it is an inferior good.
      1. For example, those with low incomes might prefer to eat cheap instant ramen, but once their income increases they might prefer going to a ramen shop or getting a more expensive but proper meal.
      2. Walmart, a lot cheaper but a little worse
  2. Population
    1. An increase in population will icnrease demand generally. A shift in subpopulations will change the demand for specific goods and services.
      1. For example, as a population ages, the demand for golf/elder care might increase
  3. Price of Substitutes
    1. A substitute is another good that can be consumed instead of the good.
    2. Your demand for a good will increase if the price of the substitute good rises, because your option is now cheaper and the better option.
    3. Your demand for a good will decrease if the price of the substitute goods decrease, as customers might want to buy the cheaper option.
    4. Example
      1. Big increase in the price of orange juice (really bad growing season), the opportunity cost for drinking apple juice will change.
      2. A decrease in the price of natural gas reduces demand for its substitute (oil)
  4. Price of complements
    1. Complements are things that go well together.
    2. Your demand for a good will increase if the price of complementary goods decreases.
    3. Your demand for a good will decrease if the price of complementary goods rises.
      1. If milk prices decrease, you would be willing to spend more on the same amount of cookies, or buy more cookies for a bigger amount.
  5. Expectations
    1. If you believe prices will rise, (or supply will decrease), you will make your purchase today, increasing today's demand.
    2. If you believe prices will fall, you will delay your purchase, decreasing today's demand.
  6. Tastes
    1. Changes in preferences can shift demand curves
      1. Companies spend billions of dollars each year in an attempt to change population preferences.
      2. Social pressure can shift the demand curve
      3. Preferences are also affected by fads and fashion trends
        1. SHEIN, fast fashion
      4. Example:
        1. Market for cigarettes. Changing information about smoking causing cancer caused the demand for cigarettes to decrease.
        2. Fidget spinners, trends, memes

Supply Curve

supply curve - function that shows how much suppliers would be willing to sell at certain prices
quantity supplied - the quantity that sellers are willing and able to sell at a particular price.

A supply curve can be read in two ways:

  • Horizontally: At a given price, how much are suppliers willing to sell
  • Vertically: To produce a given quantity, what price must sellers be paid?

Like consumers, firms choose what quantity to produce for markets based on:

  • the marginal principle
  • the cost-benefit principle, and the
  • opportunity cost principle

The opportunity cost principle means that firms marginal costs include variable costs

  • variable costs cost that vary with the quantity of output, such as labor and raw materials
  • fixed costs don't vary when the quantity of output changes, they are incurred regardless of level of output (e.g. land that's already been bought)

Rational Rule for Sellers:
Supply one more car kit if: Price >= Marginal cost

Diminishing Marginal Product

  • After reaching sufficient scale to take advantage of the productivity increases through the division of labor. Output of individual workers will depend on the other inputs available to them.
  • Some of these inputs are fixed, so additional workers mean less capital per worker, etc.
  • Thus, firms experience a diminishing marginal product
    • Fixed Resources: In the short run, at least one factor of production is fixed (e.g., capital, machinery, land). As more units of a variable input are added, these fixed resources become increasingly crowded, leading to inefficiencies.
    • Overutilization of Fixed Inputs: Initially, adding more workers to a production process with a fixed amount of capital (like machines) can increase productivity. However, as more workers are added, there comes a point where there are too many workers for the available machines, leading to workers waiting or getting in each other's way.
    • Decreasing Efficiency: When too many units of a variable input are used, the efficiency of each additional input decreases because the additional inputs do not have enough fixed resources to work with effectively.
    • Optimal Combination is Surpassed: There is an optimal combination of inputs that maximizes production. Beyond this point, the addition of more inputs leads to less efficient combinations, resulting in lower marginal product.
    • Limited Skill Set: Additional workers might not be as skilled or may require more training, further decreasing the marginal product as more labor is added.
  • With less marginal output coming from marginal inputs, the cost of adding additional units increases.
  • Our marginal cost increases with quantity.
  • Since firms increase output as long as P >= MC, they stop producing

EXAMPLE:
Caterham's decision on how many kits to make for the Seven (cars)

  • Benefit: Price (£28,990-£54,990)
  • Variable Costs: parts, materials, & labor
  • Fixed Costs: Factory, R&D expenses, and regulatory compliance (sunk costs)

EXAMPLE:
For oil, the amount it takes to produce oil for each country varies differently. For Saudi Arabia it's around $2 per barrel, for the United States a bit above $10 per barel. As the price rises, more suppliers are able to sell as they are now able to profit from selling oil. At $2.50/barrel only Saudi Arabia is able to profitably sell, at $15/barrel both the United States and Saudi Arabia are able to sell.

Shift versus Movements Along Supply Curves
  • When only the price changes (from the supplier's perspective): You're thinking about a movement along the supply curve.
  • When other factors change: You need to think about shifts in the supply curve.

Increase in supply - shifts the supply curve to the right.

  • At each price, producers are willing to sell more.
  • At each quantity, producers are willing to accept a lower price
    Decrease in supply - Shifts the supply curve to the left
Factors That Shift Supply
  • Technological Innovations and the Price of Inputs
    • Improvements in technology can reduce costs, thus increasing supply
    • A reduction in input prices also reduces costs and thus has a similar effect
  • Taxes and Subsidies
    • A tax on output has the same effect as an increase in costs, shifts left
    • A subsidy is the reverse of a tax, shifts right
  • Expectation
    • Suppliers who expect prices to increase will store goods for future sale and sell less today
      • The expectation of a future price increase therefore decreases the current supply.
      • The supply curve shifts to the left.
    • Suppliers who expect prices to decrease will try to sell as much as possible and sell more today
      • The expectation of a future price decrease therefore increases the current supply
      • The supply curve shifts to the right
  • Entry or Exit of Producers
    • The entry of new producers increases supply, shifting the curve down and to the right
    • Note that this factor only affects the market supply curve
      • Note that this isn't (producers only enter at a certain price point), if that is the case then it is movement along the curve.
  • Changes in Opportunity Costs
    • An increase in opportunity costs shifts the supply curve up and to the left
    • Firms produce up until the point where P = MC
    • They determine this marginal cost in terms of opportunity cost
      • This means considering only variable costs of production
      • This also means considering the payoff available from other uses of resources

EXAMPLE:

  • If the price of wheat increases, the opportunity cost of growing soybeans increases
  • Some farmers will shift away from producing soybeans and start producing whet
  • The supply curve shifts to the left
