Specialization, Productivity, and the Division of Knowledge

As if by treaty, by barter, and by purchase, that we obtain from one another the greater part of those mutual good offices which we stand in need of, so it is this same trucking disposition which originally gives occasion to the division of labour. In a tribe of hunters or shepherds a particular person makes bows and arrows, for example, with more readiness and dexterity than any other. He frequently exchanges them for cattle or for venison with his companions and he finds at last that he can in this manner get more cattle and venison, than if he himself went to the field to catch them. From a regard to his own interest, therefore, the making of bows and arrows grows to be his chief business, and he becomes a sort of armourer.

Trade allows people to specialize in producing goods that they are efficient at making. People enjoy different endowment of skills and factors of production. Creates lot of opportunities to increase productivity in the long run if you specialize, focus on a single thing.

First, ... the division of labour, by reducing every man's business to some one simple operation, and by making this operation the sole employment of his life, necessarily increases very much the dexterity of the workman.

A man commonly saunters a little in turning his hand from one sort of employment to another. When he first begins the new work, he is seldom very keen and hearty; his mind, as they say, does not go to it, and for some time he rather trifles than applies to good purpose.

We can produce more through trade than by individual production.

Trade allows people to specialize, or focus on a select number of things. For example, a neurosurgeon can focus on neurons, rather then focus on how food gets put on his table. Due to our limited brain space, this allows us the entire populations to have millions of people's worth of knowledge, whereas if everyone fended for themselves (no trade present) then the entire population would have the equivalent of maybe a little more then a single person's brain space.

Division of Knowledge
Without, specialization, every person has to produce their own food, clothing, and so on.

  • Each person has about the same knowledge as everybody else
  • The combined knowledge of a society is not much more than that of a single person.
    However, with specialization, knowledge is much more diverse and specialized.
  • Each person has very different kinds of knowledge to each other
  • The combined knowledge of a society is very, very large

The Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)
A table or graph indicating all the combinations of goods that an individual or country can produce with a given endowment of inputs and technology. Due to different inputs being good for different outputs, the PPF will usually have a bowed shape. We will however simplify most of our analyses by assuming a constant marginal rate of transformation.


Assumptions:

  • Abstracts the entire economy into two goods
  • Snapshot of an economy at one time

How would our PPF change with... a breakthrough in military technology? New farming techniques? An increase in population?

  • Though the PPF is a snapshot, we can shift it to indicate the effect of changes to an economy's endowment of inputs and technology.

The opportunity cost of producing each good may different defending where an economy sits along the PPF, since different resources are differently suited for producing different goods. PPF's slope will at any point show the marginal rate of transformation between the 2 different goods.

Example:
Bob is stuck on an island, and using his eight hours of the day he can either catch one fish an hour or one coconut every 30 minutes.

The opportunity cost of harvesting a coconut is the fish that he could've caught had he not been harvesting coconuts.

  • Opportunity cost of coconut = 8 fish/16 coconuts = 1/2 fish
  • Opportunity cost of coconut = 30 min/60 min = 1/2 fish
    The opportunity cost of harvesting a fish are the coconuts that he could've caught had he not been harvesting fish.
  • Opportunity cost of coconut = 16 coconuts/8 fish = 2 coconuts
  • Opportunity cost of coconut = 60 min/30 min = 2 coconuts
Absolute Advantage

Absolute Advantage - When a country can make more of the same good with less inputs then another country. Despite one country perhaps having an absolute advantage over another, they can still trade with each other to their benefit by using comparative advantage.

We can use the PPF to help formalize our understanding of how specialization happens.

  • Countries have different climates, different talents, different amount of space, so they they're better at making different goods.

Alex shipwrecks to the same island, he's bad at harvesting fish but is really good at getting coconuts. Alex has an absolute advantage in harvesting coconuts, and Bob has an absolute advantage is fishing. Obvious area for gains in trade, so they each specialize.

Comparative Advantage - By making use of different relative values and efficiencies of certain products, one can create more goods through trade and specialization.

Example 2
Bob can produce either 10 bananas, or 10 fish.
Ahn can produce either 10 bananas or 30 fish.

The max net goods is achieved when Ahn produces 30 fish and Bob produces 10 bananas.

Opportunity cost, for Bob, 1 banana = 1 fish, for Ahn, 1 banana = 3 fish.

A good trade for both of them, would be 1 banana = 2 fish. Bob can get get 2 fish from one of his bananas, where 1 banana = 1 fish originally, and Ahn can get 1 banana for 2 fish, where 1 banana = 3 fish originally.

Opportunity cost => Trading with yourself

If Ahn catches 4 fish per banana, by becoming a better fisherman, she comparatively became a worse banana gatherer. This helps Bob, who hasn't improved at all, and can now get more fish for banana at a trade of 3 fish = 1 banana.

Trade and Globalization

On a society-wide level, the gains exceed the losses.

  • MYTH: More trade will result in wages dropping, as low-income workers in other countries will take over
  • REAL: More trade will result in wages increases, as workers can focus on tasks that have higher comparative advantage, so the productivity of everyone increases.
  • Since wages are based on productivity, everyone's wages should theoretically rise
    However, income distributions will change, and transitions can be hard on individuals. People in low demand fields might have a hard time moving to high demand fields (which might require things like specific college degrees or previous experience)

People internationally might be trading very similar goods without a clear comparative advantage. This is often due to economies of scale, where countries can mass-produce something.

  • Phenomenon especially common in international trade.
